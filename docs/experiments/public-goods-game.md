## Public Goods Game

In this experiment, we conduct a variant of the Public Goods game (e.g., Ledyard, 1994). In this game, a group of participants with an initial endowment privately propose a contribution of between 0% and 100% of their endowment to a common pool. This common pool has its value multiplied by a specific value (e.g., 1.5x), which is then distributed to all participants equally, irrespective of their initial contribution.

### Procedure (Contributors)

1. Before completing the experiment, participants are introduced to the structure of the experiment (as below). We then ask a series of attention/knowledge questions to ensure comprehension of the task setup and their role as a participant.
2. Participants are told that they will be playing a version of the public goods game with three other random participants. The contributions of these participants will be used to generate a common pool and will be used to calculate and disburse bonus payments to each individual participant.
3. Each participant is told that they are being provided with an initial endowment, which they can use to contribute to a common pool. Any contributions to the common pool will be multiplied by 1.5x and then will be disbursed equally to all four grouped participants. They are then asked to propose a contribution rate of between 0% and 100%.
4. Before the participant makes a final decision, they will receive feedback from community evaluation about whether their contribution is considered appropriate.
5. After seeing the community evaluation, participants confirm their final contribution rate.

### Procedure (Community Evaluators)

1. Before completing the experiment, participants are introduced to their role as an observer who is watching individual contributors to a public goods game. As above, we provide instructions about the task they will complete and ask a series of attention/knowledge questions to ensure comprehension of the task setup and their roles.
2. After completing the introduction, participants are provided some examples of contribution rates and introduced to some reasons why contribution rates might or might not be considered appropriate.
3. They are then shown 15-25 proposed contribution rates from contributor participants and asked to provide reasons for why this contribution rate is or is not appropriate and a final evaluation of whether the contribution rate is appropriate or not. They are told that if their evaluation matches the evaluation of the majority of other community evaluators, they will receive a bonus payment.
