## Power to Take Game

In this experiment, we conduct a variant of the Power to Take Game (Bosman & van Winden, 2002). Participants are divided into three groups: A, B, and C. Groups A and B take the role of the "proposer" and the "responder", respectively.

The proposer sets a "take rate" between 0 and 100%. The responder is gifted with an initial endowment. They must send a proportion of their money corresponding to the proposed take rate. However, they can also choose to destroy between 0 and 100% of their initial endowment.

Group C participants observe a series of interactions between a proposer and a responder and must evaluate whether the proposed take rate should be considered "fair".

### Groups A and B

1. Before completing the experiment, participants are introduced to the structure of the experiment (as below). We then ask a series of attention/knowledge questions to ensure comprehension of the task setup and their roles.
2. Two participants from groups A and B will be randomly paired. 
3. The participant from group A (the "proposer") will be asked to set a "take rate" between 0 and 100% for a participant from group B (the "responder"). The responder is gifted with an initial endowment (e.g., $10). They must send a proportion of their money corresponding to the proposed take rate. However, they can also choose to destroy between 0 and 100% of their initial endowment.
4. Before A makes a final decision, A will be given feedback from a participant from group C (the community assessment) about whether their proposed take rate is considered fair.
5. A is permitted to revise their final take rate. If this take rate differs from the previous offer, it will also receive an updated community assessment.
6. In some versions of the experiment, B may be given access to the community assessment of the take rate before making their decision. In other versions, B will only see the final proposal.

### Group C

1. Before completing the experiment, participants are introduced to their role as an observer who is witnessing rounds of interactions between two other participants (A and B) in the power to take game. As above, we provide instructions about the task they will complete and ask a series of attention/knowledge questions to ensure comprehension of the task setup and their roles.
2. After completing the introduction, participants are provided some examples of take rates and introduced to some reasons why take rates might or might not be considered fair proposals.
3. They are then shown 15-25 take rates from participants from group A and asked to provide reasons for why this take rate is or is not fair and a final evaluation of whether the take rate is fair or not. They are told that if their evaluation matches the evaluation of the majority of participants in C, they will receive a bonus payment.
